Summary Writing — Part 1

Activity: Making the Right Choice

There are a variety of writing choices when you write for a specific audience or purpose: steps you might take or skip, words you might use or avoid, styles you might apply or disregard. We will examine:

  • Tone (formal or informal)
  • Formatting
  • Depth
  • Level of Review
  • Support
  • Recognition of Sources

Tone

Have I used an appropriate tone for my purpose? Appropriate words?

In which writing situation would these choices for tone be appropriate?

Formatting

Have I made my writing look the way my audience is expecting it to look?

In which writing situation would these choices for formatting be appropriate?

Depth

Have I developed ideas logically? Have I discussed them in sufficient detail? Have I answered the question or solved the problem that I was expected to address?

In which writing situation would these choices for depth be appropriate?

Level of Review

Have I ensured my writing is complete and accurate? Have I checked to make sure I’ve done everything that was asked or expected of me?

In which writing situation would these choices for precision be appropriate?

Support

Have I supported my own thinking with ideas and information from other people: scientific researchers, people with first-hand experience, or professionals in the field?

In which writing situation would these choices for support be appropriate?

Recognition of Sources

Do I give credit to another person’s words or ideas if I’m using them?

In which writing situation would these choices for recognizing sources be appropriate?
