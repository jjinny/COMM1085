Reported Speech: A Summary

You may begin teaching reported speech at an intermediate level. Reported speech has a number of subtle variations that you probably won’t touch on until your students are more advanced.

Basic Reported Speech:

When I tell you what someone else said without directly quoting, it is known as reported speech. Let’s look at an example:

The left column contains the sentences that the roving reporter, Hound Dog, wrote out in preparing to write her exposé about a contentious town hall meeting discussing Light Rail Transit.

The right column of the following chart lists quotes from a Town Hall meeting which may or may not have been held in Waterloo Region prior to the commencement of building the light rail system.

“LRT is good for Waterloo Region,” said a city counsellor. A city counsellor said that LRT was good for Waterloo Region.
“We are moving toward sustainable development,” another counsellor reminded the audience. Another city counsellor reminded the audience that they were moving toward sustainable development.
“There was not enough consultation,” one citizen said. One citizen said that there had not been enough consultation.
“The Region was not paying attention to its citizens during the planning stages”, the citizen emphasized. The citizen emphasized that the Region had not been paying attention to its citizens during the planning stages.
“We haven’t calculated the costs accurately,” one activist exclaimed. One activist exclaimed that they hadn’t calculated the costs accurately.
“An accurate estimate was calculated well before the plan was submitted,” countered a city official. A city official countered that an accurate estimate had been calculated well before the plan had been submitted.
“We will see the benefits of the LRT,” promised the Regional Chair. The Regional Chair promised that they would see the benefits of LRT.
