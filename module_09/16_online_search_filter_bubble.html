Activity:Online Searches and the Filter Bubble

So far this week we have explored two effective ways to ensure that your research will draw on reliable sources:

  1. Search databases that are curated by professional librarians
  2. Manually evaluate the quality and credibility of each source with criteria like the CARS model

Food for thought:
Today’s information landscape is even more complicated than ever before. Internet publishing tools have allowed more people than ever before to make their ideas visible, but not everybody has the skills or the ethics to present information accurately and responsibly.

Optional: Test your ability to assess the validity of online content. This Globe and Mail article defines fake news and gives some tips on how to spot fake news. Can you pass the quiz?

Optional: Although this resource provides ten questions for fake news detection, the questions are also useful for assessing any source.

Without these safeguards, searching for the information on the web can lead us to information that is flawed in a variety of ways. While the CARS model encouraged us to question the credibility and accuracy of the sources we consult, it is always important to strive for a variety of perspectives and voices in your search.

As you can see from this 2011 TED Talk, the same websites that enable us to feel more connected to information and ideas on the web may actually be restricting our ability to access a full range of perspectives. This limited access to a variety of opinions has been named a “filter bubble” by web executive Eli Pariser, who describes this growing trend in this 2011 TED talk.

Answer the following questions based on Pariser’s TED talk.

Apply to the Workplace!
Why is it important to ensure that we consult a range of perspectives when we’re attempting to answer an important question or understand a complicated question? How could your business proposal, technological design, or medical research be improved by consulting a wide range of sources?
