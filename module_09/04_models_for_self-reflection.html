Models for Self-Reflection

Now that we’ve established the purpose — the why — of self-reflection, our focus shifts to asking how: How can we narrow our focus? How can we organize the process?

This topic page describes four different methods or models for self-reflection (WriteOnline.ca and University of Waterloo’s Centre for Teaching Excellence). Although this week’s Feedback Journal assignment asks you to present your reflections in a specific way, you should think of these models as tools that you can use throughout your academic and professional career to help you reflect on the various situations you might encounter. To learn more about each model, click the title below.

What? / So What? / Now What?

The “What-So What-Now What” model was developed by Borton (1970). It’s a quick and easy way for you to reflect on a situation or project after it has happened. Bruce (as cited in Write Online, n.d.) provides a set of questions, which we’ve adapted here, that you can use to generate ideas. Click on each title for more.

The Six Sages

Brockbank and McGill (1998) suggest asking six fundamental questions when attempting to engage in reflection: What, When, Where, Who, How, and Why. To see some sample questions for each, click on the sections of the diagram.

The Compass

Seidel and Blythe (as cited in Write Online, n.d.) created a model called “the compass.” This model encourages you to look in four “directions” as you reflect on a particular event or project. For a summary ( ) of what these four “directions” signify, click each title.

Developmental Reflection

This model follows the “learning hierarchy,” which distinguishes between types of thinking and sorts them according to their complexity. So, remembering and understanding are identified as basic forms of thinking and learning, while evaluating and creating are defined as more complex. To read some sample questions to aid in your reflection, click each step of the learning hierarchy.
