Activity: Best Practices for Editing

Editing begins after you have ensured that your document overall is organized, unified, and meets the requirements of the assignment.

Editing and proofing both focus on the sentence level. Editing, however, questions and analyzes sentences, whereas proofreading only involves checking them for error (Engineering Communication Program, 2017). When editing, focus on tone, your use of quotations, and your citations.

Check for Tone

Tone is the term we use to describe the writer’s attitude to their subject or their audience. The attitude that you express in your writing should be appropriate for your purpose and audience. To check the appropriateness of your tone, look at the way you have worded or phrased important ideas.

Attendance at the meeting on Friday is mandatory, as the executive committee will be presenting its goals for the coming fiscal year, in addition to other critical things.

Reduce Length and Number of Quotations

Quoting an expert or eye-witness can provide your writing with depth and detail, but your writing can become choppy and confusing if it is filled with quotations. Avoid this mistake by quoting selectively. Use quotations only when the wording or phrasing of the passage is particularly profound or powerful. Instead, try to paraphrase the majority of your research.

Read the following student sample with a quotation integrated into the sentence.

A company which has already started to use virtual reality (VR) for their training is NASA. They are using all different types of VR to train their astronauts for the harsh conditions of space. As Gaudiosi (2016) mentions in his article, “By recreating physical locations in VR, you can simulate environments that are not easily accessible, or require extensive training” (para. 11).

Check Citations

When you submit your writing to the submission folder on eConestoga, the file is automatically reviewed by software that compares your submission to a comprehensive database of web pages and other student writing. This comparison will allow you to see which sections of your paper appear elsewhere on the web in a report referred to as a Similarity Report.

If you have submitted your assignment ahead of the assignment due date, you can review your Similarity Report to check that you have correctly followed the citation guidelines each time you have used ideas and information originally found in other sources.
