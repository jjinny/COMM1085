From Outline to Research Paper

The outline you prepare should include all of the information and ideas that your final paper will need, but it shouldn’t be written in the same style as your final paper. Instead of linking together sentences and paragraphs, you will use a condensed version of writing called “point-form” style. This style is likely familiar to you as the style that you use to take notes in class. It’s a helpful way to write quickly without sacrificing any important meaning.

We will see this week how an outline contains all the major details that lead ultimately to a research paper. Look at the comparison between the writing in this outline and the way the same ideas are represented in the final paper.

Krispy Kreme’s Failed Expansion into Canada and How it Plans to Rise Again: Outline Research Question: Why did Krispy Kreme fail in its first attempt at expansion and what lessons were learned?

Purpose Statement: The purpose of this report is to investigate the causes of Krispy Kreme’s failed expansion into Canada from 2001 to 2005, how the business plans to re-enter the Canadian market and achieve success and sustainability, and the threats that Krispy Kreme faces in this business operation.

Why Original Expansion Failed

The story of Krispy Kreme’s failed expansion into Canada can be told in three parts : high start-up and real estate costs, combined with an early peak in sales, exposed poor business planning and a lack of understanding of the Canadian market.

Unreasonable Costs

Krispy Kreme’s First attempt to expand into Canada failed due to unexpectedly high costs. Stores were too expensive to operate due to unexpectedly high upkeep and real estate costs. Stores were around 5000 square feet each (Harris, 2016, para. 3) and cost up to 2-3 million dollars to open (Shaw, 2016, para. 7). Such high fixed costs nullified revenue produced by these so called “factory outlets”, preventing Krispy Kreme’s typical sales model of relying almost exclusively on these types of stores for selling to consumers.

Inconsistent Sales

Krispy Kreme’s first attempt to expand into Canada failed partly due to sales peaking early, resulting in overproduction amid rapid expansion. Sales of donuts were initially high, but not enough to justify costs as the businesses’ novelty wore off (Harris, 2016, para. 23). Sales declined rapidly after its first few years in Canada. “There was almost too much hype” according to Keith Stein, the chairman of Krispy Kreme Canada Incorporated (Wright, 2016, para. 5)

Flawed Market Research

Another reason Krispy Kreme’s Canada expansion failed was their poor understanding of the Canadian market. Canadians do not eat as many donuts as Krispy Kreme believed, and competition was fierce (Harris, 2016, para. 21). The company did not make accurate projections of how its business model would do in Canada. For example, for Tim Hortons, Krispy Kreme’s primary competitor in the donut café industry in Canada, donuts make up only 7% of total sales (Harris, 2016, para. 14). Krispy Kreme quickly discovered that in order for a donut shop to succeed in Canada, it must offer more than just donuts.

Plans for New Expansion

Despite their failed initial attempt, Krispy Kreme intends to give the Canadian market another try. However, their second effort will differ from the first in three major areas: the number of factories, the style of their stores, and the strategy of their marketing campaign.

Limiting Factory Outlets

Krispy Kreme plans to successfully expand into the Canada by limiting its factory outlets to a small percentage of its total stores. In response to the company’s unjustifiably high start-up and operating costs of its factory outlets in Canada, Krispy Kreme plans to open as few factory outlets as possible and only increase with low real estate costs (CBC, 2016, para .25). This will save the company money, and increase responsiveness to unexpectedly high or low sales in Krispy Kreme’s other types of stores which will not produce their own donuts (Wright, 2017, para.15).

Café Model

Krispy Kreme plans to successfully expand into the Canada by relying primarily on a café-style store model. Krispy Kreme has noticed that donut companies in Canada tend to succeed under a café type business model, which emphasizes selling coffee and providing a meeting place to consumers as much as it is concerned with selling donuts (Wright, 2017, para. 8). Krispy Kreme believes this new format has a lot of potential (CBC, 2016, para. 15). The company intends to implement a hub-and-spoke style system, where a limited number of factory outlets will produce donuts which will then be delivered to surrounding café-style outlets twice a day. This will drastically decrease average start-up and operating costs for Krispy Kreme’s stores in Canada.

Exploit Brand Power

Krispy Kreme plans to successfully expand into the Canada by exploiting its brand power. Krispy Kreme has experienced massive growth recently, with sales experiencing double digit growth for the last five years (Shaw, 2016, para. 2). The company believes it has retained its brand recognition in Canada (Wright, 2017, para. 16). According to Stein, “Millennials and young people embrace this brand and they seem to think it is cool” (Shaw, 2016. para. 19). Krispy Kreme will also use social media to grow its brand recognition (Wright, 2016, para. 16)

Challenges Facing New Expansion

Krispy Kreme’s revised plan for Canadian expansion is likely to be more successfully than the first, but the company should still expect to face significant obstacles. Two challenges in particular will need to be addressed if Krispy Kreme’s second run at the Canadian donut market is going to work: inexperience and competition.

Core Competency

Krispy Kreme has little experience running café-style shops on a large scale, so the company will be operating outside of its core competency. Krispy Kreme has always relied on its ability to sell donuts to its customers right off the production line, ensuring maximum freshness. The company’s planned model for Canada mostly scraps that model, and introduces a model, “dramatically different” from the Krispy Kreme of old” which the company has not yet proven it can run on a large scale (Harris, 2016, para. 9). This model comes with new challenges, namely the logistics of shipping donuts from store-to-store and selling large quantities of coffee.

Crowded Market

Canada has the most donut shops in the world per capita, making the country’s donut market extremely fierce and competitive (CBC, 1994, para 1). Canada’s donut market does not have much room left to grow, so the country’s massive demand is already met with exceptionally high supply, and features companies aggressively guarding their market share. According to Robert Carter of the NPD Group, a Canadian market research company, “the quick service restaurant market is flat. So, for the chain to grow, it will have to steal customers from competitors” (Harris, 2016, para. 29).

Krispy Kreme’s greatest competitor in Canada is Tim Hortons, which is Canada’s most influential brand and a cultural icon (Martin, 2016, para. 4). In fact, Tim Hortons is more than just a competing business; it has become an essential aspect of Canada’s national identity. If Krispy Kreme is to expand in Canada, it must wrestle customers away from Tim Hortons (Harris, 2016, para. 29), even though the company’s leaders have “no intention of growing aggressively and compet[ing] with Tim Hortons,” according to Stein (Shaw, 2016, para. 32). Still, the company will have to match Tim Hortons in quality and it will have to overcome Canadian patriotism, a tool which Tim Hortons tends to exploit as it protects its market dominance from newcomers.

D. Conclusion

  • Krispy Kreme made mistakes in first attempt to expand into Canada
  • Can learn from its past failures and implement → can replicate some of its American success in America
  • Must overcome major challenges → operating outside of their core competencies and competing against significant opposition.

Conclusion

Krispy Kreme learned some harsh lessons in its first foray into Canada. Between the high costs of opening and operating, the inconsistency of sales, and the difficulty in understanding Canada’s complex donut market, many believed the donut giant would accept their fate focus their business elsewhere, never to try to return to the country as a major retailer again. Luckily for the many Canadians who couldn’t get enough of the glazed icon, Krispy Kreme has decided to give Canada another taste of its business in another attempt to establish itself in the great white north, constructing its business model around Canada, using a hub-and-stoke system to increase efficiency.

However, Krispy Kreme will continue to face challenging obstacles: using an untested business model will likely expose unforeseen problems, and operating in an extremely crowded market will leave little margin for error. But if Krispy Kreme can exploit its opportunities and overcome its numerous obstacles, the prize may be worth the struggle. Even if the American donut giant is able to secure only a small fraction of the Canadian market – a far more likely outcome than a total domination of the existing competition – it should still consider the expansion successful. After all, in the donut capital of the world, even a Timbit’s worth of the market is still worth a lot.
