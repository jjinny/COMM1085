Organizing Research — Part 2

Summary on Writing Outlines

The following outline from an actual student paper is a model for an excellent outline. To learn what this student did well and what the student could have improved, click on the icons.

Research Question:
What particular innovations from Moog Music allowed it to influence synthesizers - and thus remain strongly relevant - in today’s music industry?

Working Thesis (answer to the question):
Moog’s lasting success in the music industry can be attributed to these three main topics: innovative electronic engineering, layout and design, and high-profile collaborations/endorsements.

Reason it matters - Introduction context:

  • Modern electronic instruments have helped to change the sounds heard in all sorts of media, to this day: music (many genres), movies, video games, etc.
  • Keith Emerson with Emerson, Lake and Palmer (Jenkins, 2007, p. 48)
  • Films such as Blade Runner (Lendino, 2012, para. 1)
  • There is one brand, among many others, that seems to be credited with defining the ‘synthesized’ sound: Moog
  • Why is this? What did founder Robert Moog do to synthesizers, specifically, that had such a lasting effect on the music industry?

Innovative Electronic Engineering

Strong Academic Background in Electronics - Robert Moog

  • constructed his first Theremin instrument at age 14, later sold Theremin kits to pay for education (“Building on the Legacy”, 2013, p.118)
  • completed a degree in electrical engineering at Columbia University, as well as a Ph.D. at Cornell University (“Building on the Legacy”, 2013, p.119)

Voltage-Controlled Modulation

  • can be regulated in a circuit consistently; makes any sort of adjustments easier for achieving desired sound (Jenkins, 2007, p.49)
  • e.g., compared to dependency on capacitance or current from earlier engineers, which is heavily influenced by temperature, humidity, etc. (Jenkins, 2007, p.50)
  • Moog’s patents made his products much more highly desired, at this early stage (Jenkins, 2007, p. 49)
  • released first ‘modular synthesizer’ in 1964 (McNamee, 2010, para. 2)
  • demonstrated work at 1964 New York Audio Engineering Show Convention (“Building on the Legacy”, 2013, p.120)
  • introduced enough customers to seriously start business

Layout and Design Layout
  • important for how users interface with oscillator modulation
    • synths such as Minimoog helped to establish what would be considered standard ‘signal path’ (Jenkins, 2007, p. 50)
  • developed a straight-forward layout for sound manipulation (Lendino, 2012, para. 3):
    • signal generators (oscillators, pink/white noise)
    • filter (harmonics, resonance)
    • amplifier (ADSR: attack, decay, sustain, release)
  • addition of ‘mod-wheel’ (used to modulate pitch) would soon become standard on synths from future companies (“Building on the Legacy”, 2013, p.121)
  • allowed users to achieve what seemed like an endless combinations of possible tones
  • collaboration with Herbert Deutsch allowed implementation of keyboard layout on Moog’s synths (Jenkins, 2007, p.51)
    • allowed much easier use for regular musicians

Design

  • many early synths required complex, manual cable patching for individual components (Jenkins, 2007, p. 53)
    • for some, this was a turn-off as it could become very complicated
  • models such as Minimoog came pre-patched (Jenkins, 2007, p.53)
    • no manual patching with lots of confusing cases
    • perfect for average musician looking to easily incorporate synth sounds into work

Pricing

  • early synthesizers, including Moog’s 1964 modular synth, were relatively expensive for the time; outside the budget of most musicians (McNamee, 2010, para. 4)
  • Minimoog pricing was manageable; allowed musicians
  • 1970s: with marketing, got Minimoog products into more general music stores, rather than specialty electronic stores (Jenkins, 2007, p. 55)

Minimoog

  • considered by many to be the main contributing factor to Moog’s legacy ‐ came out on the market in 1970; sold 13,000 units over ten years (“Building on the Legacy”, 2013, p.122)
  • iconic sound, design, portability, and affordability

High-Profile Collaborations/Endorsements

Collaborations with Academics

  • Moog’s work caught the attention of Herbert Deutsch and Walter (later Wendy) Carlos
    Herbert Deutsch
    • professor of music at Hofstra University (“Building on the Legacy”, 2013, p. 124)
    • later became marketing manager for Moog
    • collaborated with Moog to implement keyboard layout design (Jenkins, 2007, p. 52)
    Walter (Wendy) Carlos
    • music and electronics student at Columbia-Princeton
    • collaborated with Moog while in school to make changes to existing modules
    • early Moog synths were monophonic, playing only one note at a time (no chords)
    • helped to establish multi-track techniques, using recorders, to achieve multiphonic sounds (Jenkins, 2007, p. 57)
    • in 1968, released Switched on Bach (McNamee, 2010, para. 2)
      • funding from CBS allowed Carlos to record an entire album of classical music using synthesizers; used Moog’s modular synths (Jenkins, 2007, p. 57)
      • album became widely critically acclaimed; directly contributed to expansion of synthesizer awareness with both major labels and independent artists (Jenkins, 2007, p. 57)

Collaborations with High-Profile Musicians

  • Artists: Chick Corea, Herbie Hancock, Keith Emerson, Rick Wakeman, Jan Hammer, Mick Jagger (Jenkins, 2007, p. 58)
    • Keith Emerson is often noted as the first to use Moog synths in live performance; helped bring significant exposure to mainstream audience (Jenkins, 2007, p. 58)
  • Bands: The Doors, The Monkees, The Byrds
    • Hits such as Hot Butter’s Popcorn, and Space’s Magic Fly make notable use of Moog synths (McNamee, 2010, para. 7)

Conclusion
  • Moog’s early work helped to define the synthesizer we know today, in all sorts of media
  • legacy can be contributed by these three main points
    • research and patents on voltage-control parameters
    • innovative sound, design, layout, and portability; most recognized from the Minimoog
    • collaborations and endorsements from many professional academics and highprofile musicians; Walter (Wendy) Carlos’ Switched on Bach put the Moog sound on the map.
  • it is said that innovation comes from standing on the shoulders of giants
    • current electronic music instrument designers stand on the shoulders of Moog... and it’s hopefully clearer to see why, now
    • important for future electronic music instrument designers to know their ‘origins’; hopefully know where to go with this past innovation in mind.
