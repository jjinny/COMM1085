Organizing Your Research

Last week, we introduced you to the concept of using headings and sub-headings to help you organize your research. One advantage of using headings and sub-headings is that they are flexible: it’s possible to create as many sections and in whatever pattern as you need to effectively answer your research question.

For example, your research might fit easily into a basic outline template, such as this:

Outline for [working title of your research report]

  1. INTRODUCTION:
    1. Have you met the length requirements of the assignment?
  2. BODY:

    *Note: each subsection can have several paragraphs to discuss the point. Don’t feel that you can only have one paragraph per section. The key is to thoroughly discuss each point so that it is clear to your reader.

    1. Topic sentence for first main point
      1. Explanation, proof, or example
      2. Secondary research source(s) to support the point (quotation or paraphrase), with in-text citation(s)
    2. Topic sentence for second main point
      1. Explanation, proof, or example
      2. Secondary research source(s) to support the point (quotation or paraphrase), with in-text citation(s)
    3. Topic sentence for third main point
      1. Explanation, proof, or example
      2. Secondary research source(s) to support the point (quotation or paraphrase), with in-text citation(s)
  3. INTRODUCTION:
    1. Revisit purpose statement/solution/answer/recommendation
    2. Summarize and highlight the supporting evidence
    3. End with a strong closing point that reinforces your central idea or purpose

If your research findings don’t suit this template, you can add or rearrange the headings and sub-headings to more effectively organize your materials. Review other samples of outlines provided this week to see what is possible.
