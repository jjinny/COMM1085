Summary Writing — Part 1

The Main Verb

Once you locate the subject of a sentence, you can move on to the next part of a complete sentence: the verb.

A verb is often an action word that shows what the subject is doing. However, a verb can also link the subject to a describing word or phrase (complement), or indicate additional information about the action, such as when it occurs.

Let’s focus on three types of verbs so that you can find the main verb of a sentence:

  • Action verbs
  • Linking verbs
  • Helping verbs

Action Verbs

An action verb answers the question what is the subject doing? In the following sentences, the action verbs are in bold.

  • The patient answered the ambulance driver’s questions about the accident.
  • He called his job to say he would be late for work.

Linking Verbs

A verb can often connect the subject of the sentence to a describing word or phrase (complement). This type of verb is called a linking verb because it links the subject to the word or phrase that describes the subject. In the following sentences, the linking verbs are in bold.

  • The tools were old and poorly maintained.
  • The soldering iron seemed broken.

If you have trouble telling the difference between action verbs and linking verbs, remember that an action verb shows that the subject is doing something, whereas a linking verb simply connects the subject to another word that describes or modifies the subject.

Learner Tip

A few verbs can be used as either action verbs or linking verbs.

  • Action Verb: The student looked for her graphing calculator.
  • Linking Verb: The student looked tired.

Although both sentences use the same verb, the two sentences have completely different meanings. In the first sentence, the verb describes the student’s action. In the second sentence, the verb describes the student’s appearance.

Helping Verbs

Helping verbs are verbs that are used with the main verb to add extra information to the verbs, such as when the action occurs or how likely it is to occur.

There are different kinds of helping verbs: auxiliary and modal.

Auxiliary verbs include forms of be, have, and do. Review the various forms of these auxiliary verbs below and see in the examples how the auxiliary verb is part of the main verb.

Be

Forms of be include: am, are, is, was, were, being, been

  • I am writing my test tomorrow.
  • They were walking into class when the fire alarm rang.

Have

Forms of have include: have, has, had, having

  • My professor has challenged the class to earn the highest average on the final exam.
  • We have accepted the challenge.

Do

Forms of do include: do, does, did

  • Although a deduction is applied, this course does accept late submissions.
  • Many students do use this option.

Learner Tip

It’s important to note that be, do, and have aren’t always helping verbs. Compare the following examples.

Heather is running a marathon.

  • Is is a helping verb that works together with running. The two words are the verb.

Heather is Lovepreet’s best friend.

  • Is is a linking verb. Heather is a friend to Lovepreet.

Jesse doesn’t eat meat.

  • does is a helping verb that works together with eat. The two words are the verb.

Jesse does homework in the evenings.

  • Does is an action verb. Jesse does.

Modal verbs can show how likely something is, indicate that one action depends on another, or show politeness. The most common ones are: will, would, shall, should, can, could, may, might, and must.

  • If I won the lottery, I could start a business.
  • Could you do me a favor?

Learner Tip

When you are making a main verb with a helping verb negative, the not or n’t goes on the helping verb:

  • Cannot
  • Don’t
  • Wouldn’t
