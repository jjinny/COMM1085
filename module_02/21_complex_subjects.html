Summary Writing — Part 1

Complex Subjects

Identifying the subject of a sentence can be relatively straightforward when the subject contains the types of nouns or pronouns that we’ve reviewed so far. However, the subject of a sentence can become more difficult to identify if it contains one of the following types:

  • Compound subject
  • Gerunds
  • Infinitives

Compound Subjects

A sentence may have as the subject more than one person, place, or thing linked together with and. These subjects are called compound subjects. Compound subjects are useful when you want to discuss several subjects at once.

Desmond and Maria have been working on that design for almost a year.

Books, magazines, and online articles are all good resources.

Gerunds

Gerund describes a verb that ends in –ing, but functions as a noun. Gerunds can be difficult to identify as nouns because they are formed from verbs and seem to express action or a state of being. As a noun, a gerund can function as a subject.

Studying was the key to my success on the exam.

It can be easier to see that “studying” is the subject of this sentence if we replace the gerund with a common noun:

In both examples, the bolded word is the subject, or the main focus, of the sentence.

Studying was the key to my success on the exam.
Preparation was the key to my success on the exam.

It’s also important to understand that "-ing words" do not always act as nouns. For example, here is the word "studying" acting as you might expect it to, as part of a verb: "I will be studying at the library tonight."

Infinitives

Infinitives are another verb form that can function as a noun. Infinitives are relatively easy to spot in a sentence because they follow a basic formula: the word ‘to’ + a verb (in its most simple form), for example, to + study = “to study”. As a noun, an infinitive can be a subject.

I understand this concept now. To study any longer would be unnecessary.

It can be easier to see that "to study" is the subject of this sentence if we replace the infinitive with a word that we're already comfortable identifying in that role:

In both examples, the bolded word is the subject, or the main focus, of the second sentence.

I understand this concept now. To study any longer would be unnecessary.
I understand this concept now, so I won’t study any longer. That would be unnecessary.
