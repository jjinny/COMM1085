Summary Writing — Part 1

The Audience for Academic Writing

The second fundamental question — who’s the audience for academic writing? — is equally important because it will shape some of the decisions you make as a writer.

Understanding your audience and your purpose is clearly an important early step toward successful academic writing. Often the instructions of a writing assignment will help you to confirm what you’re aiming to achieve and who you’re trying to communicate with.

Obviously, in every instance of academic writing your audience will include your instructor who will assess whether your work meets the assignment expectations. The audience might also include, depending on the assignment:

  • Your classmates — if the assignment requires you to display or present your information
  • A hypothetical reader — the person or type of person that you might be writing for in a professional context, such as clients or customers, suppliers or vendors, and colleagues or managers


Learner Tip!

It’s common for students to overlook the assignment instructions when they’re eager to begin writing. Using details from the assignment, the instructions, and the rubric, if available, can help you to understand the purpose and audience of an assignment.

Here are some common places to look for assignment directions in eConestoga:

  • A module called Evaluations
  • In the module for the week when the assignment is due, for example, Week 6
  • In Assignments in the Course Tools menu
  • In Rubrics in the Course Tools menu
