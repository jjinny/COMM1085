Summary Writing — Part 1

Integrating Quotations

Quoting is one way to use someone else’s ideas in your own writing. Although quotations generally capture the writer’s exact words, sometimes it’s necessary to make minor changes to the wording without changing the writer’s meaning.

APA@Conestoga style allows you to make four kinds of changes to quotations:

  • Changing punctuation
  • Changing the first letter
  • Omitting information
  • Adding information

Changing Punctuation

The punctuation mark at the end of the sentence from the original source can be changed to fit your sentence.

Original:

What this all means for human workers is not yet clear. Some economists say the displaced will simply find new occupations, as has historically been the case when a new technology comes along. Plus, there are all those engineering and programming jobs needed to design and build automated systems. But a growing number are convinced that technological change now moves with such speed that jobs are being automated away in greater numbers than new ones can be realized. The phenomenon is even being used to explain the unique characteristics of today’s economy—everything from stubborn unemployment to the ballooning class of low-paid, low-skilled workers.

(Sorenson, 2013, para. 4)

Quotation:

The effects of automation on employment are difficult to manage because"technological change now moves with such speed that jobs are being automated away in greater numbers than new ones can be realized" (Sorenson, 2013, para.4).

Changing the First Letter

The first letter of the first word from the source can be changed to an upper or lower case letter to fit your sentence.

Original:

What this all means for human workers is not yet clear. Some economists say the displaced will simply find new occupations, as has historically been the case when a new technology comes along. Plus, there are all those engineering and programming jobs needed to design and build automated systems. But a growing number are convinced that technological change now moves with such speed that jobs are being automated away in greater numbers than new ones can be realized. The phenomenon is even being used to explain the unique characteristics of today’s economy—everything from stubborn unemployment to the ballooning class of low-paid, low-skilled workers.

(Sorenson, 2013, para. 4)

Quotation:

According to Sorenson (2013), "what this all means for human workers is not yet clear" (para. 4).

Omitting Information

To leave out part of a sentence, replace the group of words with an ellipsis: ...

Original:

What this all means for human workers is not yet clear. Some economists say the displaced will simply find new occupations, as has historically been the case when a new technology comes along. Plus, there are all those engineering and programming jobs needed to design and build automated systems. But a growing number are convinced that technological change now moves with such speed that jobs are being automated away in greater numbers than new ones can be realized. The phenomenon is even being used to explain the unique characteristics of today’s economy—everything from stubborn unemployment to the ballooning class of low-paid, low-skilled workers.

(Sorenson, 2013, para. 4)

Quotation:

According to Sorenson (2013), "the phenomenon is even being used to explain…everything from stubborn unemployment to the ballooning class of low-paid, low-skilled workers" (para. 4).

Ellipses are only necessary when you want to exclude information from the middle of a quotation. You do not need to insert ellipses to indicate that you started your quotation in the middle of the original sentence. In the same way, you do not need to insert ellipses to indicate that you ended your quotation before the original sentence concluded.

Original:

What this all means for human workers is not yet clear. Some economists say the displaced will simply find new occupations, as has historically been the case when a new technology comes along. Plus, there are all those engineering and programming jobs needed to design and build automated systems. But a growing number are convinced that technological change now moves with such speed that jobs are being automated away in greater numbers than new ones can be realized. The phenomenon is even being used to explain the unique characteristics of today’s economy—everything from stubborn unemployment to the ballooning class of low-paid, low-skilled workers.

(Sorenson, 2013, para. 4)

Quotation:

Starting in the middle of a sentence:

The effects of automation on employment are difficult to manage because "technological change now moves with such speed that jobs are being automated away in greater numbers than new ones can be realized" (Sorenson, 2013, para.4).

Ending in the middle of the sentence

According to Sorenson (2013), "some economists say the displaced will simply find new occupations" (para. 4)

Adding Information

To add information such as an explanation, place added material within square brackets like this: [ ]

Original:

What this all means for human workers is not yet clear. Some economistssay the displaced will simply find new occupations, as has historically been the case when a new technology comes along. Plus, there are all those engineering and programming jobs needed to design and build automated systems. But a growing number are convinced that technological change now moves with such speed that jobs are being automated away in greater numbers than new ones can be realized. The phenomenon is even being used to explain the unique characteristics of today’s economy—everything from stubborn unemployment to the ballooning class of low-paid, low-skilled workers.

(Sorenson, 2013, para. 4)

Quotation:

According to Sorenson (2013), "a growing number [of economists] are convinced that technological change now moves with such speed that jobs are being automated away in greater numbers than new ones can be realized" (para. 4).
