Summary Writing — Part 1

Locating Subjects

Subjects often appear at or near the beginning of sentences in English. In fact, the examples we’ve used to discuss subjects so far all feature the subject at the beginning of the sentence:

  • Malik is the project manager for this project.
  • He will give us our assignments.
  • Books, magazines, and online articles are all good resources.
  • Studying was the key to my success on the exam.
  • To study any longer would be impossible.

However, subjects do not always appear at the beginning of the sentence. When you’re trying to identify the subject of a sentence, it’s important to understand the following:

  • Prepositional phrases
  • Introductory "it" or "there"

When a sentence begins with a prepositional phrase, the subject is no longer at the beginning. It can also be a challenge to identify the subject of the sentence when the sentence begins with any of the following: it is, it was, there is (was), there are (were).

Prepositional Phrases

As we saw in the parts of speech video {insert quicklink to topic: Parts of Speech}, prepositions are words that tell us how nouns and pronouns interact with time and space.

Some common prepositions include:

In

Through

At

From

Under

For

With

On

"Prepositional phrase" describe a group of words that begins with a preposition and includes a noun, pronoun, or words acting like a noun. Prepositional phrases provide extra or non-essential information. For example:

  • Harpinder left his iPhone in the library.
  • The fastest way to the E-wing is through the cafeteria.
  • When she arrived at class, all of the chairs were taken.

For more details on each prepositional phrase, click on the icon.

Those examples featured prepositional phrases that appear at the end or in the middle of a sentence, but prepositional phrases can also appear at the beginning of a sentence. When a prepositional phrase appears at the beginning of a sentence, it can appear as though it contains the subject. But the subject of a sentence is never located in a prepositional phrase.

Review the following sentences. Can you identify the prepositional phrase and the subject of the sentence? Check your answers by clicking on the icon.

With strong communication skills, I can advance more quickly in my career.

Before the internet, people had fewer opportunities to publish their own ideas.

Around the world, information is travelling at the speed of light.

Introductory "it" or "there"

Identifying the subject can also become complicated when the sentence begins in one of the following ways:

  • There is (was) / are (were)
  • It is / was

There are students lining up around the building to catch the bus.

It was the snowstorm that delayed the bus.

Identifying the subject in a sentence that begins this way can be difficult because it might seem as though “there” or “it” are the subjects in these sentences. After all, we are used to finding subjects in front of the verb in English sentences.
