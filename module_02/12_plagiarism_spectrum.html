Summary Writing — Part 1

The Plagiarism Spectrum

Plagiarism takes many shapes. The following "Plagiarism Spectrum" identifies 10 types of plagiarism based on a survey of secondary and post-secondary instructors (Turnitin, 2012).

Click on each title to learn the characteristics of the various types of plagiarism and how plagiarism is not just cheating, but it is also ineffective writing.

Clone

Clone plagiarism occurs when you submit someone else’s work, word-for-word, as your own. In the example below, the plagiarized portion is underlined.

Click the arrow to compare with the original

New Version

Out of 702 job categories examined in the Oxford study, 47 per cent were deemed possible candidates for some degree of future automation. The shift is being driven by technological advancements in key areas where computers have typically lagged humans. One is perception. Thanks to improved sensor technology, engineers are now able to build robots that are capable of functioning outside of strictly controlled environments, such as factories.

Original Text

Out of 702 job categories examined in the Oxford study, 47 per cent were deemed possible candidates for some degree of future automation. The shift is being driven by technological advancements in key areas where computers have typically lagged humans. One is perception. Thanks to improved sensor technology, engineers are now able to build robots that are capable of functioning outside of strictly controlled environments, such as factories.

(Sorenson, 2013, para.5)

In this case, the writer copied the whole paragraph and inserted it without changes into their paper.

Why it doesn't work

The purpose and audience of the original text will likely be different from its new setting. For example, the original might respond to a specific historical event, use a style that is appropriate to another context, answer a different question, etc.

CTRL-C

The CTRL-C sample of plagiarism contains significant portions of text from a single source without alterations. In the example below, the plagiarized portion is underlined.

Click the arrow to compare with the original

New Version

In a recent study, 47 per cent of jobs were deemed possible candidates for some degree of future automation. The shift is being driven by technological advancements in key areas where computers have typically lagged humans. It’s easy to find examples of this trend in today’s news. Google's driverless car, for example, relies on a combination of GPS, lasers, radar and sophisticated software to help it plot its course and avoid obstacles along the way. Imagine how this new technology will affecting everyone from taxi drivers to long-haul truckers.

Original Text

Out of 702 job categories examined in the Oxford study, 47 per cent were deemed possible candidates for some degree of future automation. The shift is being driven by technological advancements in key areas where computers have typically lagged humans. One is perception. Thanks to improved sensor technology, engineers are now able to build robots that are capable of functioning outside of strictly controlled environments, such as factories. Google’s driverless car, for example, relies on a combination of GPS, lasers, radar and sophisticated software to help it plot its course and avoid obstacles along the way. In fact, Frey and co-author Michael Osborne predict that transportation and logistics will be one of the first sectors to be hit by the next wave of automation, affecting everyone from taxi drivers to long-haul truckers.

(Sorenson, 2013, para.5)

In this case, the writer strung together different sentences, taking the words from the original passage out of context.

Why it doesn't work

The writer combines parts of the text without capturing their original relationship. Google’s driverless car is offered as an example of "key areas where computers have typically lagged," but in the original, it’s offered as a specific example of the perception that new computer programs can perform.

This kind of cut and paste can also leave obvious "cut lines" between the source text and the new context. Notice the awkward grammar of the phrase "will affecting" in the final sentence.

Find-Replace

When the writer attempts to paraphrase the original text but only changes a few key words and phrases, retaining the essential content of the source, plagiarism has still occurred. In the example below, the plagiarized portion is underlined.

Click the arrow to compare with the original

New Version

Out of 702 kinds of jobs looked at in the Oxford study, 47 per cent were considered possible candidates for some amount of future automation. The change is being forced by technological advancements in key areas where computers have usually been slower than humans. One is perception. Thanks to better sensor technology, engineers are now able to build robots that are capable of working outside of serious environments, such as factories. Google's driverless car, for example, relies on a combination of GPS, lasers, radar and sophisticated software to help it find its way and avoid obstacles along the way.

Original Text

Out of 702 job categories examined in the Oxford study, 47 per cent were deemed possible candidates for some degree of future automation. The shift is being driven by technological advancements in key areas where computers have typically lagged humans. One is perception. Thanks to improved sensor technology, engineers are now able to build robots that are capable of functioning outside of strictly controlled environments, such as factories. Google’s driverless car, for example, relies on a combination of GPS, lasers, radar and sophisticated software to help it plot its course and avoid obstacles along the way.

(Sorenson, 2013, para.5)

In this case, the writer replaced only a few words. The remainder of the passage is copied word for word. This doesn't constitute an effective paraphrase.

Why it doesn't work

When you substitute synonyms, the writing can become awkward or — even worse — incorrect because the meaning changes as well. "Strictly controlled", for example, is not the same as "serious" in the new version.

This example also introduces repetition and sloppiness, for example, the repetition of "way" in the final sentence: "find its way" and "along the way".

Remix

A remix occurs when the writer takes paraphrases from multiple sources and then fits them together. In the example below, the plagiarized portions are underlined.

Click the arrow to compare with the original

New Version:

A recent Oxford study found that almost half of all jobs are at risk of being automated. Henry Siu, a professor at the University of British Columbia who specializes in automation and the decline of middle-class jobs has predicted that the economic impact of these job losses could be huge. But, MIT economist Erik Brynjolfsson suggests that history should give us reason to be optimistic. He thinks automation might create new jobs, just like industrialization did. And, he says, this might create new forms of wealth.

Original Text:

Passage 1:

Out of 702 job categories examined in the Oxford study, 47 per cent were deemed possible candidates for some degree of future automation.

(Sorenson, 2013, para. 5)

Passage 2:

I spoke to Henry, Siu, a professor at the University of British Columbia who specializes in automation and the decline of middle-class jobs; he predicted that the trucking industry could automate within the span of one to two years. The economic changes this would spark cannot be understated. In the United States, this could mean the loss of eight to nine million jobs, and mostly from lower-income families, further exacerbating economic disparities. Worse yet, according to Siu, this shift would most likely come in the midst of a recession, when the industry is looking for new ways to cut costs.

(Gilmore, 2017, para. 7)

Passage 3:

Erik Brynjolfsson is less pessimistic. An MIT economist who co-authored The Second Machine Age, he thinks automation won’t necessarily be so bad. The Luddites thought machines destroyed jobs, but they were only half right: They can also, eventually, create new ones. “A lot of skilled artisans did lose their jobs,” Brynjolfsson says, but several decades later demand for labor rose as new job categories emerged, like office work. “Average wages have been increasing for the past 200 years,” he notes. “The machines were creating wealth!”

(Thompson, 2017, para. 38)

Each section is paraphrased or summarized from three original texts. As paraphrases and summaries standing alone, each is well done. The problem here is the lack of citation. Even paraphrases and summaries need to be cited.

Why it doesn't work

This is closer to good academic writing, but without citations, it denies the reader the opportunity to join the conversation, i.e., to read the things you’ve read.

Recycle

A recycled passage borrows generously from the student’s previous work. In the example below, the plagiarized portion is underlined.

Click the arrow to compare with the original

New Version:

Submitted to ECON1030: Macroeconomics

Automation is the future trend that will have the biggest effect on the economy. One widely cited study estimated that almost half of all jobs will be affected by automation (Frey & Osborne, 2013, p. 1). Automation will require large segments of the workforce to retrain. However, the shockwaves caused by this change may not be felt equally: lower income families will likely be hurt worst when industries begin to adopt automation more widely (Gilmore, 2017, para. 7).

Original Text:

Submitted to COMM1086: College Reading and Writing

As traditional jobs become automated at a faster rate, the effects will be felt throughout the economy. One widely cited study estimated that almost half of all jobs will be affected by automation (Frey & Osborne, 2013, p. 1). However, the shockwaves caused by this change may not be felt equally: lower income families will likely be hurt worst when industries begin to adopt automation more widely (Gilmore, 2017, para. 7).

The student has taken part of a paper written for a previous course and included it in a paper they are submitting to their current course.

Why it doesn't work

While the original is well constructed and well-documented, it’s recycled in the second context. The connections in the submission to ECON1030 between the examples and the main ideas aren’t as logical, e.g., the need to retrain is not fully supported by the evidence about lower income families.

Hybrid

The hybrid type of plagiarism combines perfectly cited sources with copied passages without citation. In the example below, the plagiarized portion is underlined.

Click the arrow to compare with the original

New Version:

As traditional jobs become automated at a faster rate, the effects will be felt throughout the economy. For example, Out of 702 job categories examined in the Oxford study, 47 per cent were deemed possible candidates for some degree of future automation. However, the shockwaves caused by this change may not be felt equally: lower income families will be hurt worst when industries begin to adopt automation more widely (Gilmore, 2017, para. 7).

Original Text:

Out of 702 job categories examined in the Oxford study, 47 per cent were deemed possible candidates for some degree of future automation. The shift is being driven by technological advancements in key areas where computers have typically lagged humans.

(Sorenson, 2013, para. 5)

One passage is cited correctly, but the sentence from Sorenson, with the same words, is not.

Why it doesn't work

Copied sources are often easily apparent because the writer has neglected to modify punctuation or capitalization. Also, when text is copied from an online source, the font or the size of the text may not be changed to match the rest of the paper.

Also, it’s important to notice how the focus is out of balance here. It’s not helpful to focus so closely on the details provided in this copied text, i.e., exactly how many job categories were studied. The reader would have appreciated a paraphrase that presents the main idea of this example

(Thompson, 2017, para. 38)

Passages are put side by side without any context from the writer and with no citation of the original sources.

Why it doesn't work

This passage includes references to ideas explained elsewhere in the original texts, but aren’t clear in this version, e.g., What Oxford study? Who are the Luddites?

404 Error

A 404 Error means that a site on the internet cannot be found. This type of plagiarism, therefore, includes citations to non-existent sources or the citations contain inaccurate information. In the example below, the plagiarized portion is underlined.

New Version:

Out of 702 job categories examined in the Oxford study, 47 per cent were deemed possible candidates for some degree of future automation (Source A). The shift is being driven by technological advancements in key areas where computers have typically lagged humans. One is perception (Source B). Thanks to improved sensor technology, engineers are now able to build robots that are capable of functioning outside of strictly controlled environments, such as factories. Google's driverless car, for example, relies on a combination of GPS, lasers, radar and sophisticated software to help it plot its course and avoid obstacles along the way (Source C). In fact, Frey and co-author Michael Osborne predict that transportation and logistics will be one of the first sectors to be hit by the next wave of automation, affecting everyone from taxi drivers to long-haul truckers (Source D).

Original Text:

Out of 702 job categories examined in the Oxford study, 47 per cent were deemed possible candidates for some degree of future automation. The shift is being driven by technological advancements in key areas where computers have typically lagged humans. One is perception. Thanks to improved sensor technology, engineers are now able to build robots that are capable of functioning outside of strictly controlled environments, such as factories. Google’s driverless car, for example, relies on a combination of GPS, lasers, radar and sophisticated software to help it plot its course and avoid obstacles along the way. In fact, Frey and co-author Michael Osborne predict that transportation and logistics will be one of the first sectors to be hit by the next wave of automation, affecting everyone from taxi drivers to long-haul truckers.

(Sorenson, 2013, para. 5)

Often, this type of plagiarism is done to make unreliable or irrelevant sources seem more appropriate. The resulting combination of materials tends to be confusing and unconvincing.

Why it doesn't work

Copied sources are often easily apparent because the writer has neglected to modify punctuation or capitalization. Also, when text is copied from an online source, the font or the size of the text may not be changed to match the rest of the paper.

Also, it’s important to notice how the focus is out of balance here. It’s not helpful to focus so closely on the details provided in this copied text, i.e., exactly how many job categories were studied. The reader would have appreciated a paraphrase that presents the main idea of this example

Aggregator

Includes proper citation to sources but the paper contains almost no original work. In the example below, the plagiarized portion is underlined.

Click the arrow to compare with the original

New Version:

As traditional jobs become automated at a faster rate, the effects will be felt throughout the economy. "Out of 702 job categories examined in the Oxford study, 47 per cent were deemed possible candidates for some degree of future automation" (Sorenson, 2013, para. 5). "The economic changes this would spark cannot be understated. In the United States, this could mean the loss of eight to nine million jobs, and mostly from lower-income families, further exacerbating economic disparities" (Gilmore, 2017, para. 7). Erik Brynjolfsson is less pessimistic. An MIT economist who co-authored The Second Machine Age, he thinks automation won't necessarily be so bad. The Luddites thought machines destroyed jobs, but they were only half right: They can also, eventually, create new ones” (Thompson, 2017, para. 38). As you can see, automation is a complicated topic.

Original Text:

Passage 1:

Out of 702 job categories examined in the Oxford study, 47 per cent were deemed possible candidates for some degree of future automation. The shift is being driven by technological advancements in key areas where computers have typically lagged humans.

(Sorenson, 2013, para. 5)

Passage 2:

I spoke to Henry, Siu, a professor at the University of British Columbia who specializes in automation and the decline of middle-class jobs; he predicted that the trucking industry could automate within the span of one to two years. The economic changes this would spark cannot be understated. In the United States, this could mean the loss of eight to nine million jobs, and mostly from lower-income families, further exacerbating economic disparities. Worse yet, according to Siu, this shift would most likely come in the midst of a recession, when the industry is looking for new ways to cut costs.

(Gilmore, 2017, para. 7)

Passage 3:

Erik Brynjolfsson is less pessimistic. An MIT economist who co-authored The Second Machine Age, he thinks automation won’t necessarily be so bad. The Luddites thought machines destroyed jobs, but they were only half right: They can also, eventually, create new ones. "A lot of skilled artisans did lose their jobs," Brynjolfsson says, but several decades later demand for labor rose as new job categories emerged, like office work. "Average wages have been increasing for the past 200 years," he notes. "The machines were creating wealth!"

(Thompson, 2017, para. 38)

The writer took correctly-cited quotes from a variety of sources and put them side by side without any commentary.

Why it doesn't work

This kind of writing typically fails to achieve the purpose of the assignment. It may present relevant and reliable information, but it hasn’t been brought together and explained in a way that answers the original question or arrives at an original conclusion.

Retweet

This type of plagiarism is similar to the “Find-Replace” error, as both fail to paraphrase the original text sufficiently. However, in this example, the writer has attempted to cite the original author by including a citation at the end of the paragraph. In the example below, the plagiarized portion is underlined.

New Version:

Out of 702 kinds of jobs looked at in the Oxford study, 47 per cent were considered possible candidates for some amount of future automation. The change is being forced by technological advancements in key areas where computers have usually been slower than humans. One is perception. Thanks to better sensor technology, engineers are now able to build robots that are capable of working outside of serious environments, such as factories. Google's driverless car, for example, relies on a combination of GPS, lasers, radar and sophisticated software to help it find its way and avoid obstacles along the way (Sorenson, 2013, para. 5).

Original Text:

Out of 702 job categories examined in the Oxford study, 47 per cent were deemed possible candidates for some degree of future automation. The shift is being driven by technological advancements in key areas where computers have typically lagged humans. One is perception. Thanks to improved sensor technology, engineers are now able to build robots that are capable of functioning outside of strictly controlled environments, such as factories. Google’s driverless car, for example, relies on a combination of GPS, lasers, radar and sophisticated software to help it plot its course and avoid obstacles along the way.

(Sorenson, 2013, para. 5)

In this case, the writer replaced only a few words. The remainder of the passage is copied word for word. The source is cited, but the extent of the quotation is not indicated, even though it is treated as though it were a paraphrase.

Why it doesn't work

This kind of writing often becomes disorienting and incomprehensible, as the words that are chosen to replace the original words tend to distort the original meaning of the text.
