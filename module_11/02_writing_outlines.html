Organizing Research — Part 1

Writing Outlines

After your professor approves your research proposal, it’s time to begin gathering the ideas and information that you’ll use to answer your research question.

You may be tempted to begin writing your paper right away, crafting the sentences and paragraphs that you’ll ultimately present to your reader, but it’s important to delay the actual writing stage until you’ve produced a detailed and organized outline.

An effective outline contains all of your own ideas and all of the information that you need to support your ideas, and it is arranged in a way that will allow you to write your paper more easily and more effectively.

Remember how you asked a question in your proposal. This week we will discuss how to move from the question on the proposal to a purpose or a thesis statement and an outline. Here is an example of a question from a proposal about the expansion of Krispy Kreme into the Canadian market.

Original question (from proposal): Will Krispy Kreme’s new expansion into the Canadian market be profitable enough to allow the business to survive long-term in Canada?

While this question is headed in the right direction, it needs improvement because it can be answered with only a yes or no. With feedback from the teacher, the student has revised the question to ask: Why did Krispy Kreme fail in its first attempt at expansion and what lessons were learned.

Let’s look at the final purpose statement and the outline for the Krispy Kreme expansion research paper, which builds on the points in the purpose statement. The student identified three reasons for Krispy Kreme’s failure and the outline provides support for each reason.

Outline

  • the causes of Krispy Kreme’s failed expansion into Canada:
    • unreasonable costs
    • inconsistent sales
    • flawed market research
  • the company’s strategy for succeeding in its second expansion
    • building fewer factory outlets
    • adopting a café style for its retail stores
    • promoting their brand more aggressively
  • the challenges the company must still overcome for its expansion to be successful
    • its inexperience in running a café-style store
    • stiff competition from Tim Horton’s

The value of the outline stage in the writing process is sometimes underappreciated, but it is a valuable skill to master, whether the end product is a written paper, a website, a home renovation, or whatever you may be working toward.
