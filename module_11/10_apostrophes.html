Apostrophes

There are two main uses of the apostrophe:

  1. Contractions: To indicate that one or more letters have been removed to form two words into one.
  2. To indicate ownership or possession

Possession

While the use of apostrophes to form contractions is relatively familiar to most post-secondary students, using apostrophes to indicate ownership or possession can become complicated.

The basic concept is fairly straightforward: For singular nouns, use an apostrophe and the letter s to indicate possession.

placeholder for circles

The use of possessive apostrophes becomes more complicated, however, when there are multiple owners / possessors.

How do we combine the plural ‘s’ on the owners with the possessive ‘s’? Place the apostrophe after the plural ‘s’ and delete the possessive ‘s’:

  • The students’ essays (the essays of several students)
  • The companies’ products (the products of several companies)
  • The customers’ purchases (the purchases of several customers)

Words that indicate they are plural without an ‘s’ use the singular form of possession, that is, apostrophe followed by an ‘s’:

  • The children’s coats
  • The people’s votes
  • The women’s change room
  • The men’s change room

But what about words that already end in ‘s’ — words like ‘business’ or names like ‘Francis’?

    If the word or name is singular, then we keep the possessive ‘s’ after the apostrophe.
  • The business’s accountant said that taxes are going up. (one business)
  • Francis’s car broke on the way to work.
    • If the word is plural, then the apostrophe is placed where you might expect: drop the extra ‘s’ that would otherwise appear after the apostrophe:
  • The businesses’ taxes were increased.

We never use apostrophes to indicate simple plural forms. Each of the following examples illustrates the correct use of a plural ‘s’.

  • Two employees were late.
  • Two companies shut down over Christmas.
  • Several families went on vacation together.

The same is true for acronyms and numbers:

  • He bought several TVs.
  • I prefer Macs over PCs.
  • Movies from the 1990s are my favourite.

Possessive Adjectives

Another common apostrophe mistake involves using a possessive apostrophe with certain pronouns. English features eight possessive pronouns that don’t require an apostrophe to indicate possession — in fact, these words are used exclusively to indicate possession and have no other role in the language.
