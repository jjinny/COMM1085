1. Facebook’s new system works by allowing apps or web sites to store an account recovery “token” on Facebook’s servers. 2. When a user turns the feature on the service pushes that token to Facebook via the user’s browser in an HTTPS-encrypted connection. 3. From then on, if at any point the user forgets his or her password, or loses a device used for two-factor authentication, they can retrieve the token by proving their identity to Facebook, and then use it to recover access to the account they were locked out of.

4. Facebook’s identity-proving process uses more than just a password. 5. It can require inputting a temporary code texted to the user’s phone, checking that the token’s being retrieved from a known device in a familiar location, and even requiring the person to fill out Facebook’s so-called Social CAPTCHA, which requires them to identify pictures of friends within a time limit. 6. “The idea is to leverage those indicators Facebook has and let you prove that you’re still you,” says Hill. 7. “Account recovery isn’t something that happens every day, so it’s not a big deal to add a little friction to that process to keep it secure.”

8. Like practically every action Facebook takes, Delegated Account Recovery will no doubt raise suspicions. 9. Its possible to see it as an effort to gain a tighter hold of your online activities, or gather more data by spying on your linked accounts. 10. But Hill says: Facebook designed the system to not let Facebook learn anything from the account recovery tokens other than which service a user has linked. 11. The service encrypts the token so that only the partnered service, not Facebook; can identify the specific account being recovered.
