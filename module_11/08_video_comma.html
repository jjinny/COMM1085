Video: Comma

Knowing when and where a comma is needed in a sentence can be one of the most troublesome aspects of writing for many students.

  • Perhaps you feel confident about their use in certain places, but not in others.
  • Perhaps you have been given non-technical strategies, such as: “use a comma when you want to create a pause in your writing.”
  • Or maybe you’re completely unsure about their use and you feel like you’re simply shaking them on your writing like sprinkles on an ice cream sundae.

We have already seen in week 5 how we use commas to separate dependent clauses from independent clauses and how commas work with the FANBOYS conjunctions (For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So) to separate independent clauses. For some good memory tricks about how commas work here, watch this video.

After you have watched the video, you may choose to answer these review questions.

To separate items in a series

"When you want to list several nouns in a sentence, you separate each word with a comma. This allows the reader to pause after each item and identify which words are included in the grouping. When you list items in a sentence, put a comma after each noun, then add the word and before the last item.” (Writing for Success, Chapter 3.1, “Commas in a List of Items”)"

The renovation plans included upgrades to the cafeteria, library, welcome centre, and athletic centre.
The renovation plans included upgrades to the cafeteria, library, welcome centre and athletic centre.

Both examples are correct. The only difference between the two sentences is the comma after welcome centre. This final comma in a series (known as the “serial” or “Oxford” comma) is optional but often used. In certain cases, the meaning of a sentence is ambiguous if the serial comma is not used.

Imagine, for example, that a pop star wins an award, and, in the news the following day, the report notes her acceptance speech: She thanked her parents, Taylor Swift and Kanye West. Without the comma after “Taylor Swift”, the sentence is ambiguous.

  • One possible interpretation is that the pop star thanked only two people — her mother (Taylor Swift) and her father (Kanye West). This interpretation is correct if the pop star’s parents are Taylor Swift and Kanye West.
  • The other possible interpretation is that the pop star thanked four people — her two parents and the two other pop stars.

Using the serial comma, on the other hand, removes any ambiguity about the meaning of the sentence: She thanked her parents, Taylor Swift, and Kanye West.

  • According to this punctuation, the only possible interpretation is that the pop star thanked four people — her two parents and the two other pop stars.
To set off introductory material

“You may notice a comma that appears near the beginning of the sentence, usually after a word or phrase. This comma lets the reader know where the introductory word or phrase ends and where the main sentence begins.” (Writing for Success, Chapter 3.1, “Commas after an introductory word or phrase”)

Unfortunately, the materials we need for that project are out of stock.

To set off interrupters

“In conversations, you might interrupt your train of thought by giving more details about what you are talking about. In a sentence, you might interrupt your train of thought with a word phrase called interrupting words. Interrupting words are separated from the rest of the sentence by commas. You can determine where the commas should go by looking for the part of the sentence that is not essential for the sentence to make sense.” (Writing for Success, Chapter 3.1, “Commas before and after interrupting words”)

Agriculture, especially the growing of wheat, was vital to Canada’s economy during World War I.

You can tell that commas are necessary around the phrase “especially the growing of wheat” because the sentence still makes sense without those words: Agriculture was vital to Canada’s economy during World War I.

This kind of interrupting phrase adds detail and depth to the reader’s understanding of a topic.

To set off a direct quotation

When introducing a direct quotation or reported speech, insert a comma after the signal phrase and before the quotation mark:

The student admitted, “I’ve always wanted to have a better understanding of commas.”

For dates, some addresses, numbers, and openings and closings of letters

Content from Writing for Success, Chapter 3.1, “Commas in dates, addresses, and the greetings and closings of letters”

“You also use commas when you write the date, such as in cover letters and e-mails. Commas are used when you write the date, when you include an address, and when you greet someone.

If you are writing out the full date, add a comma after the day and before the year

I will write my last exam on May 15, 2014.

“You do not need to add a comma when you write the month and day or when you write the month and year”

My birthday is November 23.
I visited Switzerland in July 2011.

“If you need to continue the sentence after you add a date that includes the day and year, add a comma after the end of the date”

I intend to graduate on May 28, 2020, so I have plenty of time to plan what I’m going to wear to the convocation.

“Greeting are also separated by commas. When you write an email or letter, you add a comma after the greeting word or the person’s name”

Hello,
Dear Mrs. Al-Sayf,

“You also need to include a comma after the closing (the word or phrase you place before your signature)”

Thank you,
Sincerely yours,

“You also use commas when you include addresses and locations. When you include an address in a sentence, be sure to place a comma after the street and after the city. Do not place a comma between the province or state and the postal or zip code. Like a date, if you need to continue the sentence after adding the address, simply add a comma after the address.”

The Doon campus of Conestoga College is located at 299 Doon Valley Drive, Kitchener, Ontario N2G 4M4.
After moving to Cambridge, Ontario, Xing used public transit to get to school each day.
