Persuasive Writing — Part 2

Bias and Credibility

When people suggest that an argument is biased, they are likely suggesting that the argument is unfair and should be rejected because the person making the argument is only capable of looking at the topic from one limited perspective.

Bias is a way of looking at a situation, person, group, etc., through the lens of personal preferences, relationships, stereotypes, and affiliations rather than an objective analysis. In fact, almost all arguments include at least some bias because, after all, we all see the world through our own eyes and our own set of experiences. Such bias can affect your credibility if it leads you to overlook important information or other points of view.

Therefore, we need to acknowledge our personal perspectives in order to prevent a bias that affects our credibility with our audience.

Think of the many factors that might have an influence on the things you care about, the things you value:

  • gender
  • age
  • nationality
  • language
  • education
  • ethnicity
  • religion
  • job
  • socio-economic position
  • cultural traditions

Even if we prevent these factors from completely shaping our argument, they still are likely to influence our sense of what’s important or what’s right and wrong.

Acknowledging Personal Perspective

It’s impossible to eliminate all traces of our subjective experience; instead of trying, we can try to make them more visible to ourselves and our reader. There are two ways we can do this: explicitly and implicitly.

Acknowledging bias explicitly

It may be necessary to directly identify or explain how you view or approach this topic:

  • Do you know the people involved?
  • Have you been in the past or are you currently a member of the group, culture, profession etc. that you’re discussing?
  • Do you stand to benefit or suffer — financially, professionally, legally — from the outcome of this discussion?
  • Do you have an emotional connection to the topic — e.g. were you or someone that you know hurt or affected by the people or issue you’re discussing?

Acknowledging bias implicitly

Even if we don’t have a direct relationship or connection to the topic we’re discussing, our perspective might guide our research on the topic. This influence might lead us to instinctively seek out perspectives that reinforce our own.

To counteract this instinct, strategically seek out a wide range of voice and perspectives. Presenting your reader with a variety of perspectives can reduce the impact of your own bias and signal to your reader that you are making an effort to be objective. We will talk about this more in week 9.

How to Limit Bias in Writing

In your previous experience with academic writing, you have probably encountered a “rule” that the writer should avoid the use of first-person “I”.

This principle is generally useful in attempting to limit our bias — when we refer to ourselves as the author of the argument, we encourage the reader to focus on us rather than on the evidence we are presenting to support our claims.

Take these two sentences as an example.

I believe screen time for children should be limited.

In this case, the writer may be inviting the reader to question the credibility of the writer of the argument more than the legitimacy of the argument itself. Does this writer have children? Does the writer have parental help? Does the writer also have a job?”

Also, by stating this position as a personal belief, the writer might make readers feel judged; perhaps they already feel guilty about the amount of screen time their child has.

I believe screen time for children should be limited.

This sentence is more neutral and objective. It shifts the focus from the writer to the argument, so the reader is now less likely to question the writer’s bias and more likely to question the writer’s evidence. The reader may still be wary of the claim, but they are still likely to be more receptive than they would have been if the writer had presented the claim as a personal opinion.

Feeling “I” vs. Thinking “I”

Although the previous example demonstrates the problems caused by the use of “I” in persuasive writing, it is important to recognize the difference between a first-person perspective based on “feeling” and one that is based on “thinking”.

Feeling “I”

“Feeling I” asks us to relate to the writer on a personal level, because they are sharing their subjective preferences

  • I like / dislike…
  • I love / hate…
  • I feel…
  • I believe…
  • I prefer…

Thinking “I”

“Thinking I” refers to the writer in a limited way, as the author of this idea or argument

  • I argue…
  • I claim…
  • I contend…
  • I agree / disagree…
  • I suggest…
  • I propose…

Referring to yourself in the first-person in the context of these “thinking” verbs is acceptable in persuasive writing, because you won’t lose credibility with the reader. In fact, it’s preferable to the use of awkward alternatives, such as referring to yourself in the third-person, e.g., “This writer claims…” or in the passive voice, e.g., “It is suggested that…”.

If a reader perceives that the writer’s bias has overwhelmed the argument, they are less likely to take the writer or the argument seriously. It’s a reasonable reaction, similar to the one we all might have if we suspect that we are playing a game with somebody who’s cheating, or if we were buying vegetables at the grocery store and we realized the check-out clerk was putting his thumb on the scale to drive up the cost of the purchase.

If, on the other hand, the writer can acknowledge the limitations of his or her own perspective and look beyond them, then there is no reason to doubt the author’s credibility — the argument should still be evaluated on the merits of its logic and persuasive power.
