Engaging the emotion of readers can be an effective tool in persuasion, but it is a tool whose success relies on the audience and the purpose of the text. Tone is the term we use to describe the author’s attitude toward the subject and the audience. In fact, when we’re writing, tone is the only tool we have to tell our readers if we’re serious or joking, concerned or optimistic, surprised or disappointed. We change our tone with the words we choose (diction) and the order we place them in (sentence variety).

Sentence Variety

Read the following passage to observe what you can achieve when you intentionally vary the length of sentences:

This sentence has five words. Here are five more words. Five-word sentences are fine. But several together become monotonous. Listen to what is happening. The writing is getting boring. The sound of drones. It’s like a stuck record. The ear demands some variety.

Now listen. I vary the sentence length, and I create music. Music. The writing sings. It has a pleasant rhythm, a lilt, a harmony. I use short sentences. And I use sentences of medium length. And sometimes when I am certain the reader is rested, I will engage him with a sentence of considerable length, a sentence that burns with energy and builds with all the impetus of a crescendo, the roll of the drums, the crash of the cymbals — sounds that say listen to this, it is important.

So write a combination of short, medium, and long sentences. Create a sound that pleases the reader’s ear. Don’t just write words. Write music.

Gary Provost, quoted in Clark, R.P., (2008), page 31

Diction

In academic writing, the term diction simply refers to word choice. Successful writers match their diction to their audience and purpose. Often, an idea can be expressed in multiple ways, and so we must consider which particular words will have the desired effect

Using the three C’s of writing is the best way both to establish your authority and to engage your readers’ emotions when writing persuasively.

  • Concise: Readers appreciate when writers get right to the point. Don’t feel compelled to use multiple words when just a few will do.
  • Clear: Readers also value clarity. Avoid using overly academic or technical language unless it’s necessary.
  • Correct: Although your readers want you to be concise and clear, they still expect your choice of words to be accurate and precise. Vague or imprecise language can distort your meaning if you’re not careful.

A good way to be concise, clear and correct is to use specific words. Using specific words can illustrate what you mean quickly and can also engage the readers’ emotions.

Verbs

If you want to show the reader that you are confident in your position, use strong verbs rather than nouns or vague verbs.

Instead of: This email is a response to the client’s original question.
Write: This email responds to the client’s question.

Instead of: This proposal has many flaws.
Write: This proposal contains many flaws

Instead of: I will look into how Krispy Kreme failed to enter the Canadian market.
Write: I will explore how Krispy Kreme failed to enter the Canadian market.

Adjectives

General words like ‘great’, ‘nice’, and ‘beautiful’ fail to produce a specific image in the reader’s head. Using detailed adjectives when we are referring to the quality of someone or something becomes almost like a form of objective proof.

For example, here’s how the Student’s Oxford Canadian Dictionary suggests we might replace the word ‘nice’ in various contexts:

  • a delightful / splendid / enjoyable time
  • a satisfying / delicious / exquisite meal
  • a fashionable / stylish / elegant outfit
  • a cozy / comfortable / attractive room

Avoid too much emotion

Techniques that we may apply in informal settings to engage emotions will be ineffective when you are trying to write to persuade.

  • Avoid shouting or ranting: Using ALL CAPS is an acceptable way to indicate your emotion in informal online communication, but it would undermine your credibility in a formal persuasive. Likewise, we don’t use boldface, italics, or underline in formal writing to express emphasis. Our words alone are expected to convey our sense of frustration or outrage, if those emotions are a necessary component of your argument
  • Avoid insulting your reader or hypothetical opponents: Formal writing conventions also preclude us from using expressions that might be appropriate in casual speech, like those which might use sarcasm, exaggeration, or exasperation:
    • No sane person could believe
    • It is obvious that
    • Anyone can see
    • Are you kidding me?
