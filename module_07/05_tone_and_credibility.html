Persuasive Writing — Part 2

Tone and Credibility

In writing, tone is the term that describes a writer’s choice of words (diction) and sentence structure.

When we communicate with others through writing, we must rely entirely on our words to achieve our purpose. Without the use of body language or facial expressions to emphasize and reinforce our ideas, the tone of our writing becomes an extremely valuable tool – especially when our purpose is to persuade our reader. The writer’s tone reflects their attitude both to the topic and to the reader.

When you are writing persuasively, you want to present yourself to the reader as a credible source of ideas. The more your writing sounds like everyday speech — or communication that you might have with people that you’re already familiar with — the less your reader is going to view you as an authority whose argument they should hold in high esteem.

Some elements on informal language that you should avoid include:

  • Colloquialism
  • Slang
  • Idiom
  • Cliché
  • Contractions

This paragraph is an example of writing that is too informal.

I’m on transit all the time, which means I’m making all kinds of contributions to society, like traffic and the environment just to name a few. So, it’s high time my government said thank you for all the great things I do just by riding the bus rather than taking my car. But, instead they totally did the opposite with this budget. Are you kidding me?! By taking away the tax credit that saved me serious coin on my tax refund, what they’re showing me is that they don’t really mean what they say about helping students and things like that.
