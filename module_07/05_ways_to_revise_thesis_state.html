Practice: Ways to Revise a Thesis Statement

Because the thesis statement is responsible for presenting our main idea, we want it to be precise and strong. A strong thesis statement is not created instantaneously; instead, it is developed in stages. After you form a preliminary thesis statement, look to make the following revisions:

  • Replace vague words with more precise words
  • Replace linking verbs with action verbs
  • Make the supporting points parallel

Replace vague words with more precise words

Watch out for broad and general terms, such as people, society, technology, or life. Try to focus on specific aspects or segments of that idea. It can be helpful to ask the five W (+1 H) questions: who, what, where, when, why, how. Read the working thesis below. Replace linking verbs with action verbs

Linking verbs are forms of the verb to be, a verb that simply states that a situation exists. Linking verbs often make thesis statements weak because they do not express action. By contrast, action verbs present the writer’s analysis more forcefully.

Working Thesis:

Smartboard technology is beneficial eye icon to learning in the 21st century post-secondary classroom.

See an improved version of the thesis statement

Smartboard technology benefits learning in the 21st century post-secondary classroom.

You may have noticed that the working thesis is also a bit vague – how does smartboard technology benefit learning in the 21st century post-secondary classroom?

In the 21st century post-secondary classroom, Smartboard technology engages technology-savvy students, reinforces digital presentation skills, and supports active learning strategies.

Make the supporting points parallel

A thesis statement can lose clarity if it does not have “parallelism”. Parallelism refers to the relationship between equal (or coordinate) elements: all the elements must be presented in a way that is grammatically identical

Working Thesis:

To increase sales a business must be ambitious, organized, and stay in control. eye icon

See an improved version of the thesis statement

To increase sales a business must be ambitious, organized, and in control.

Working Thesis:

To increase sales a business must communicate with existing customers and the customer base needs to be expanded. eye icon

See an improved version of the thesis statement

Parallel phrases: To increase sales a business must communicate with existing customers and expand its customer base.
Parallel clauses: To increase sales a business must set two goals: existing customers need to be engaged, and the customer base needs to be expanded.

Here is an example to illustrate how to create parallel structure:

I like to jog, swim, and hiking.

This sentence presents three activities that the speaker likes to do, but the sentence has faulty parallelism because the three activities are not expressed in the same way grammatically.

  • To jog
  • Swim
  • Hiking

Since the three items are all different grammatical forms, there are three ways to make this sentence parallel. None of these options is necessarily better than the others. You can decide which option best suits the situation. If you are interested in a grammatical explanation for each option, click the eye icon.

  • I like jogging, swimming, and hiking. eye icon
  • I like to jog, swim, and hike. eye icon
  • I like to jog, to swim, and to hike. eye icon
