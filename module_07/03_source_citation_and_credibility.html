Persuasive Writing — Part 2

Source Citation and Credibility

In week 2, we looked at two different ways to cite sources within a paper Citing and Referencing in APA@Conestoga in week 2: standard and split. Both standard and split citation style communicate important information to your reader:

  • They alert your readers to the fact that information has come from someone other than you.
  • They provide your readers with the information they’ll need to consult the source of your information if they want to check the credentials of the source or use the source in their own writing.
Although these styles achieve the same fundamental purpose in your writing, they also have individual qualities that make each one more useful in particular situations.

Let’s look at each one in more detail.

Standard Citation

In a standard in-text citation, the full citation comes at the end of the quote, paraphrase, or summary. For example:

Around-the-clock news coverage forces media producers to constantly search for headline-worthy stories. When they report outrageous stories of home gadgets malfunctioning, viewers assume that these incidents occur regularly. But that impression would be false; in fact, “of the millions of Americans who own at least one connected device, only a small fraction has come forward as victims of malicious home-gadget attacks” (Conniff, 2014, p. 60).

The standard citation is effective when:

  • The reader is not likely to recognize the name of the source
  • You want to integrate the quoted material more seamlessly into your own sentence
  • You have already used the split citation style in a previous sentence, so the reader is aware of the source
Conniff and her publication, Time Magazine, are reliable sources of information according to the checklist in the previous topic page on establishing credibility. But a glance at Conniff’s previous bylines reveals no particular area of specialization. Therefore, in this case, the standard citation style is sufficient and appropriate.

Split Citation

In a split in-text citation, the author and year of publication introduces the quote, paraphrase, or summary. For example:

Globe and Mail health reporter Andre Picard (2015) challenged recent reports that claimed processed meats are as dangerous as tobacco and asbestos, calling the claim “highly misleading” (para.9).

The split citation style is effective when:

  • You want to draw attention to the credibility of your source, e.g., your reader will recognize and trust them (or their publication) by name
  • You want to emphasize to your reader that you are drawing on an expert rather than your own experience or opinion
  • You want to present contrasting opinions from within a community of experts
Highlighting Picard’s name in the split style could help this writer establish credibility with the reader, if the reader is aware of Picard’s reputation as a leading voice in reporting on Canadian health care. Even if the reader was not aware of Picard’s expertise, the explicit reference to him might prompt your reader to search his biography — and, if they did, they would learn about his extensive experience, his awards and recognition, and links related to reporting on public health issues.

Knowledge Check

The following excerpt comes from the “Changing Nature of Cybercrime” page on Interpol’s website:

“Criminal organizations turning increasingly to the Internet to facilitate their activities and maximize their profit in the shortest time. The crimes themselves are not necessarily new – such as theft, fraud, illegal gambling, sale of fake medicines – but they are evolving in line with the opportunities presented online and therefore becoming more widespread and damaging.”
