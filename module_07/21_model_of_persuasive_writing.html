To the decision-makers and game-changers at Tim Hortons: I am writing to propose an improvement to the “Roll Up The Rim” promotion your coffee company is currently running. As you may be aware, coffee is the number one beverage of choice in Canada (Coffee Facts, 2016, para 1). Canadians may love their coffee, but nobody likes losing; and one thing that frequently leaves a bitter aftertaste is rolling up the rim to see “Please Play Again”. I propose that you consider revising your existing rules to allow your customers the opportunity to trade in 5 “Please Play Again” cups (whole cups, not just tabs) in exchange for a free timbit. I believe that this amendment will not only benefit your company, but your customers and the environment as well.

Your company is a model of success, and although your CEO may not be sitting in the board room agonizing over how to attract more customers, I do think he might want to consider how this promotion makes your existing customers feel. Happy customers are loyal customers, and by allowing them to redeem 5 otherwise losing cups to earn a free timbit, will not only ensure their taste buds are tickled, it may help to form new dining habits that last long after the promotion is finished. When one considers the long-term advantages to this slight adjustment, it is almost certain that Tim Hortons will benefit from this minor change.

Your customer base also stands to benefit. No longer will “Please Play Again” be followed by disappointment. Instead, your valuable customers will have a sense of ownership over their “win” because they will have the opportunity to work for it by collecting the required 5 cups in exchange for a timbit. It is a small shift that takes the sting out of a losing cup, and will make your customers feel like every cup has the potential to be a winner.

The environment will win as a result of these changes. Take a look around; Tim Horton’s coffee cups are a large source of litter in our cities, parks and highways. In addition, many municipalities do not currently offer recycling of coffee cups and many are forced to be sent to landfill instead of recycling facilities. (CBC News, 2016, para 1). If Tim Hortons salvaged even a small percentage of their coffee cups, these cups could then be sent to the proper facilities for recycling.

This simple change has the potential to make a big difference. Not only will your company benefit financially from the continued loyalty of your happy customers, the environment will be helped by the re-channelling of used cups to proper facilities for recycling. All this, for the cost of a .20 cent timbit, simply reconfirms that small things can create big changes.
