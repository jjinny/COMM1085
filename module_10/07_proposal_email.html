Proposing Research

The Proposal Email

Before you proceed with your research, it’s important that you include your professor, who will guide you through the process. Once you’ve developed and refined your research question, you will present your professor with a proposal of your research plan.

Your goal here is to present your professor with the information they’ll need to determine if your proposal is headed in a positive direction. They’ll look for ways to help you revise your research question and offer suggestions that can help you as proceed.

To be successful on this week’s assignment, follow the template we’ve provided here. Note that the rubric for this week’s assignment is geared toward your use of this template. For more guidance on what to include in the various elements of the email, click on each title below the template.

Since this is imitating an email, you will format your document differently than APA@Conestoga style.

  • Single-spacing
  • No indentation of paragraphs
  • Short, concise paragraphs

  • TO:
    (my myconestogac.on.ca address)
  • FROM:
    (your myconestogac.on.ca address)
  • DATE:
    (use North American standard — e.g. November 7, 2016)
  • SUBJECT:
    Proposal to study insert topic here

Opening greeting,

The opening paragraph clearly states the purpose of this email.

The first body paragraph explains the research topic and its relevance to your field of study or academic interest.

The second body paragraph explains the research question you plan to answer and names the sources you’ve read or gathered for this question. It also explains the type of research assignment you plan to write – process analysis, cause-and-effect analysis, persuasive argument etc. — and why it’s a suitable format for your topic.

The third body paragraph explains your plan for this research project — that is, how do you intend to break up the work into manageable units or stages, what internal timelines will you set for each stage, who you will ask you to help you proofread a draft, etc.

The closing paragraph clearly states your request for approval and/or raises questions or issues related to this project. Make sure you close with a direct request to proceed.

Complimentary sign-off
(your name)

Subject Line

The subject of this email is your proposal to study a particular topic, not the topic itself. Feel free to use this template for your subject line: Proposal to study [insert topic here].

Opening Greeting

This email is professional, so you should avoid casual greetings like “Hi” or “Hey”. You might decide that a more formal greeting, such as “Dear Professor” or “Greetings”, is appropriate. However, you might also simply write “Hello” or “Hello Professor.”

Opening Paragraph

The purpose of this email is to present your instructor with your proposed research question and to receive their approval and feedback.

First Body Paragraph
  1. Explain research topic
    Briefly introduce the general topic or subject that you’re exploring. You don’t need to include background information about the topic the way that you would in the opening paragraphs of your actual research paper.
  2. Explain the relevance of your topic to your field of study
    Try to identify relevant and meaningful connections, which might include the following:
    • Professional trends
    • Current / looming problems in the field
    • Potential areas of growth
    • Potential areas for employment within the field
    Try to avoid making shallow or vague explanations such as “I like it” or “I know a lot about it.” Don’t assume your professor will see how your proposed topic will overlap field of study without explaining how or why it might be worthwhile.
Second Body Paragraph
  1. Identify Your Research Question
    Remember to actually present this in the form of a question that you intend to answer rather than a general topic or focus.

  2. Potential Sources

    The purpose of this section is to confirm that you know where to look for reliable information and to confirm that there is a sufficient amount of relevant supporting material available. You can show that you know where to find reliable and relevant information by identifying the title of individual texts or the titles of particular journals or magazines that might help you answer your question.

    Try to avoid making shallow or vague explanations such as “I like it” or “I know a lot about it.” Don’t assume your professor will see how your proposed topic will overlap field of study without explaining how or why it might be worthwhile.

  3. Type of Research Question
    This section helps you to consider how your research question will lead both your research and your report in a particular direction. It will also help your instructor work with you to revise your question if necessary. You can use this table to help you think about the kind of question you’re asking:
    Table 1. Adapted from Norton, Green & Dynes, 2015, Publisher's materials.
    Example of Question Type of Question
    How is ‘x’ made or done?
    How does ‘x’ work?    		 Process
    What are the main kinds of ‘x’?
    What are the component parts of ‘x’?
    What are the significant features, characteristics, or functions of ‘x’?    		 Classification/ Division
    What are the causes of ‘x’?
    What are the effects of ‘x’?    		 Cause/ Effect
    What are the similarities and/or differences between ‘x’ and ‘y’? Comparison
    What are the main advantages/disadvantages of ‘x’?
    What are the reasons in favour of/ against ‘x’?    		 Argument/ Persuasion
Third Body Paragraph

In this section, you’ll prepare a detailed timeline for the completion of the remaining components of the research project. This timeline needs to be specific, and include some or all of the following:

  • Due dates of all relevant project completion dates (outline, rough draft, final)
  • best days of the week / times of the day for working on this assignment
  • potential conflicts, e.g. other assignments, job / family responsibilities

Closing Paragraph

This final paragraph can be brief, just like the opening paragraph. Remember to conclude with a clear request for approval and feedback. If you have any specific questions or concerns that you hope your professor will address, you can include them here.

Complimentary Sign-off

As with the greeting, you should determine the level of formality that is appropriate for your audience. Traditional sign-offs, such as “sincerely” or “yours truly,” might be appropriate in a more formal relationship. It’s also acceptable to end with a sample “Thank you” or “Kind regards” if your relationship with your audience is more casual or familiar. Note, however, that “Thanks” is likely too informal for this kind of communication.
