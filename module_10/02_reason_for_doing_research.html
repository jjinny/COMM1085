Proposing Research

Reasons for Doing Research

Research looks for answers to questions that are not easy to answer.

  • What are the potential mental health risks to a career as a police officer?
  • What areas of manufacturing are likely to benefit most from robotics technology in the next 10 years?
  • How will Artificial Intelligence technology affect the marketing industry?
  • How will quantum computing technology affect the video game industry?
  • How have welding techniques changed over the past 20 years and what can these developments tell us about the future of that trade?

“When you perform research, you are essentially trying to solve a mystery.”

For more on the purpose of research, check out this chapter in Writing for Success.

You may be able to answer these questions to some degree, but these answers will likely be based on opinions. To answer them fully and factually, you’ll need to conduct research.

Whether you are a tradesperson, a software engineer, a digital marketer, or a security officer, your job will likely require you to look into something that you do not know the answer to. You may be expected to locate relevant information, analyze your findings, organize the ideas, and then share your results with clients, co-workers, or managers. This desire to answer questions leads us to conduct research in our personal and professional lives, as well as in our academic studies.

Your research project will allow you to practice doing research by developing your own question and pursuing a line of study that is specifically relevant to your own professional and personal interests.

This kind of kind of self-directed research can be both rewarding and challenging in different ways.
